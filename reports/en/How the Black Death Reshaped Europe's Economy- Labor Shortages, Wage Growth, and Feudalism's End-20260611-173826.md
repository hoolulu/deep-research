## 1. Core Thesis

> The Black Death (1347-1352) was an exogenous shock that broke feudalism, setting Europe on a high-wage path.

### 1.1 Executive Summary

The ~40% population loss [(1)](#ref1) shifted factor endowments. Labor scarcity gave serfs outside options, reduced coercion, and drove wage growth [(3)](#ref3).

| Mechanism | Pathway | Outcome |
|-----------|---------|---------|
| Demographics | 40% loss → land-labor ratio ↑ | Labor scarcity [(1)](#ref1) |
| Institutions | Options ↑ → coercion ↓ | Wage growth [(3)](#ref3) |
| Self-reinforcement | Urbanization + war + trade | High-income state [(2)](#ref2) |

Table 1: Causal chain.

Three Horsemen model: urbanization, war, trade locked in gains [(2)](#ref2). Real wages +50-100%, urbanization ~10% to ~15-20% [(1)](#ref1)[(2)](#ref2).

| Variable | Pre-Plague | Post-Plague |
|----------|-----------|-------------|
| Real wages | Stagnant | +50-100% |
| Urbanization | ~10% | ~15-20% |

Table 2: Structural shift [(1)](#ref1)[(2)](#ref2).

Campbell: plague hit both supply and demand; institutions mediated [(5)](#ref5). City data: disruption then per capita gains [(4)](#ref4).

### 1.2 Cross-Perspective

Three frameworks: Postan/Domar (land-labor ratio) [(3)](#ref3), Three Horsemen (endogenous dynamics) [(2)](#ref2), Campbell (institutions) [(5)](#ref5).

| Framework | Mechanism | Prediction |
|-----------|-----------|------------|
| Postan/Domar | Land-labor ratio | Wage ↑ ∝ mortality [(1)](#ref1)[(3)](#ref3) |
| Three Horsemen | Urbanization-war-trade | Persistent wage [(2)](#ref2) |
| Institutional | Capacity | Heterogeneous [(5)](#ref5) |

## 2. The Pre-Plague Economy: Malthusian Equilibrium

> Pre-plague England was locked in a Malthusian equilibrium: population pressure drove wages to subsistence, fragmented holdings, and exhausted marginal soils.

### 2.1 Feudal Land-Labor System

The economy rested on manorial self-sufficiency: serfs bound to the land owed labor to lords for subsistence plots, blocking labor mobility [(9)](#ref9). Population peaked at ~6 million around 1300, straining agricultural resources [(7)](#ref7). Holdings fragmented, yields fell, soil exhaustion spread [(8)](#ref8).

| Indicator | c. 1300 |
|-----------|---------|
| Population (M) | 6.0 |
| Cultivated (M acres) | 9.5 |
| Acres/capita | 1.58 |

Table 1: Land pressure [(7)](#ref7)[(8)](#ref8).

### 2.2 Cross-Perspective

Scholars debate the severity of Malthusian pressure. Clark (2007): real wages at ~75% of early-century peak [(6)](#ref6). Postan: rents rose, wages fell, peasant welfare declined [(8)](#ref8). Broadberry: GDP/capita ~$800, stagnant 1270–1348 [(10)](#ref10).

| Period | Wage Index | % Decline |
|--------|------------|-----------|
| 1300–09 | 82.4 | — |
| 1320–29 | 75.1 | −8.9% |
| 1340–48 | 68.3 | −17.1% |

Table 2: Wage erosion [(6)](#ref6).

Three mechanisms reinforced the equilibrium: diminishing returns on fixed land, institutional rigidity from serfdom, and regressive surplus extraction enriching lords [(8)](#ref8)[(9)](#ref9).

Despite regional variation—southern England more commercialized—aggregate productivity remained stagnant [(10)](#ref10).

## 3. Demographic Collapse and Labor Scarcity

> The Black Death eliminated 40-60% of Europe's population in four years, creating labor scarcity that permanently restructured capital-labor power balances.

### 3.1 Mortality Scale

Aggregate European mortality reached ~50%, with extreme variation. A 261-site radiocarbon study confirms high mortality in Scandinavia, France, western Germany, Greece, and central Italy, while Ireland, Iberia, and central/eastern Europe saw negligible impact [(11)](#ref11).

| Region | Mortality | Source |
|--------|-----------|--------|
| Scandinavia | 60%+ | [(12)](#ref12) |
| France & Italy | 50-60% | [(12)](#ref12) |
| England | 40-45% | [(12)](#ref12) |
| S. Netherlands | ~10% | [(15)](#ref15) |

| Factor | High | Low |
|--------|------|-----|
| Urbanization | Dense | Sparse |
| Trade | Mediterranean | Atlantic periphery |

Urban centers were hit harder than rural [(14)](#ref14). The S. Netherlands "light touch" shows dense trade amplified transmission [(15)](#ref15).

### 3.2 Cross-Perspective

The shock was selective. Survivors showed 30% lower mortality risk post-plague, indicating the plague culled frail individuals [(13)](#ref13).

| Cohort | Risk | Source |
|--------|------|--------|
| Pre-plague | 1.0 | — |
| Post-plague | 0.7 (−30%) | [(13)](#ref13) |

Labor shortages drove up real wages, empowering peasants. But effects were asymmetric: high-mortality zones catalyzed institutional change (e.g., English Peasants' Revolt), while low-mortality zones maintained feudalism. The collapse was a differential institutional shock—harder-hit regions transformed deeper, widening early modern European divergence.

## 4. The Wage Revolution and Living Standards

> Plague triggered revolutionary resource redistribution toward labor, but gains varied across time and space.

### 4.1 Wage Trends

Real wages did not rise immediately—inflation in the 1340s-1370s swamped nominal gains; recovery began in the late 1370s [(16)](#ref16). English building craftsmen's real wages more than doubled, peaking in 1476-80 at levels unsurpassed till 1886-90 [(17)](#ref17). Nominal wages rose ~200% between 1347 and the 1380s; real wages doubled by mid-15th century [(18)](#ref18).

| Period | Real Wage | Source |
|---|---|---|
| 1340s pre-plague | Baseline | [(18)](#ref18) |
| 1380s post-plague | Nominal +200%; real ~2x | [(18)](#ref18) |
| 1476-80 peak | 2x+, unmatched until 1886-90 | [(17)](#ref17) |

### 4.2 Cross-Perspective

London laborers rose from 0.75x poverty line to 1.33x by 1450 [(19)](#ref19). NW Europe sustained these gains into the 19th century; continental workers collapsed back [(19)](#ref19). Wage-rent ratio doubled, triggering sumptuary laws as elite response [(25)](#ref25). Urban real wages across western Europe stayed above pre-plague until the late 16th century [(20)](#ref20).

Wealth inequality contracted for ~100 years. Florence Gini fell ~0.72 to 0.62; Germany 0.66 (1350) to 0.56 (1450), then rose again [(22)](#ref22)[(24)](#ref24). Supports Scheidel's "Great Leveler": only catastrophic shocks substantially reduce inequality [(23)](#ref23).

## 5. Institutional Unraveling of Feudalism

> The manorial system collapsed in NW Europe but intensified in the East post-Black Death, driven by demographic shock, state-building, and factor endowments.

### 5.1 Manorial System Breakdown

Post-plague labor scarcity broke the English manorial model. Lords could not prevent out-migration; by 1500 copyhold/leasehold replaced serfdom, with 80–90% of servile tenants converted [(26)](#ref26)[(29)](#ref29).

**Table 1: English Serfdom Decline**

| Period | Tenure | Labor Control |
|--------|--------|--------------|
| Pre-1348 | Villeinage | High |
| 1350–1400 | Transitional | Declining |
| By 1500 | Copyhold/leasehold | Minimal |

Serfdom collapsed fastest where population loss >40%; persisted where shocks were weaker [(30)](#ref30).

### 5.2 Cross-Perspective

Ogilvie: three determinants — state centralization, factor endowments, agrarian structure [(28)](#ref28). NW Europe: strong monarchies allied with towns. East: states imposed second serfdom to finance military expansion [(27)](#ref27).

**Table 2: Regional Paths (c. 1500)**

| Region | Serfdom | Driver |
|--------|---------|--------|
| England | Collapse | Scarcity + law |
| W/C Europe | Decline | Town alliance |
| E Europe | Intensified | Noble reliance |

**Table 3: Determinants (Ogilvie)**

| Factor | West | East |
|--------|------|------|
| Centralization | High | Low |
| Land/labor | Scarce labor | Abundant land |
| Structure | Individual | Estate farming |

Acemoglu & Robinson: strong shocks enabled change; weak preserved extraction [(30)](#ref30). Peters: Eastern state-building required serfdom, making divergence self-reinforcing [(27)](#ref27)[(28)](#ref28).

## 6. Social Conflict and Legislative Reaction

> The 1351 Statute of Labourers was England's first wage-control law; its failure reveals the limits of legislative repression.

### 6.1 Elite Repression

The Statute (25 Edw. 3) froze wages at 1346 levels, building on the 1349 Ordinance [(31)](#ref31). It restricted worker movement, fined both parties, and appointed justices of labourers [(32)](#ref32). Rothbard argues these controls defied economic law: labor scarcity beat legislation [(33)](#ref33).

| Metric | Finding |
|--------|---------|
| Baseline | 1346 wages [(31)](#ref31) |
| Enforcement | Bilateral fines [(32)](#ref32) |
| Outcome | +100-200% wages [(34)](#ref34) |

Lords shifted to sheep pasture. Wages rose 100-200% [(34)](#ref34). Three poll taxes followed (1377-1380); the 1380 flat 3-groat tax was regressive [(35)](#ref35).

| Year | Tax | Effect |
|------|-----|--------|
| 1377 | Graduated | Moderate |
| 1379 | Graduated | Building |
| 1380 | Flat rate | Revolt [(35)](#ref35) |

### 6.2 Cross-Perspective

About 100,000 rebels marched on London under Wat Tyler and John Ball, demanding an end to serfdom and poll taxes [(36)](#ref36). The revolt was suppressed: Tyler killed, concessions retracted [(37)](#ref37).

| Lens | View |
|------|------|
| Marxist | Class struggle vs landowner controls [(38)](#ref38) |
| Fiscal | Flat-rate tax made revolt inevitable [(35)](#ref35) |
| Long-run | Defeat but serfdom decline accelerated [(39)](#ref39) |

Though crushed, the revolt showed repression has limits. After 1381, poll taxes were abandoned and serfdom faded [(37)](#ref37)[(39)](#ref39). Legislative repression cannot beat structural economics.

## 7. Long-Term Economic Transformation and Legacy

> Black Death labor scarcity permanently rewired Europe's economy, incentivizing mechanization and setting the stage for the Industrial Revolution.

### 7.1 Capitalist Transition

Voigtländer & Voth show high wages drove demand for manufactures, urbanization, and permanent gains [(40)](#ref40). Clark counters agricultural efficiency stagnated 1210–1500; expansion was extensive (more land/worker), not intensive [(41)](#ref41).

| View | Mechanism | Outcome |
|------|-----------|---------|
| V&V [(40)](#ref40) | Wages → demand → urbanization | Permanent gains |
| Clark [(41)](#ref41) | Extensive margin | No productivity gain |

Labor scarcity incentivized the printing press (1440s), blast furnace, deep plow [(42)](#ref42), accelerating innovation [(43)](#ref43). Allen argues NW Europe's labor-scarce economy—traceable to the Black Death—drove coal-powered machinery adoption and the Industrial Revolution [(44)](#ref44).

| Innovation | Effect |
|------------|--------|
| Printing press [(42)](#ref42) | Replaced scribes |
| Deep plow [(42)](#ref42) | Fewer workers/acre |

### 7.2 Cross-Perspective

Clark's continuity and the transformation view converge: extensive expansion generated disposable incomes → demand for manufactures → urbanization → innovation.

| Dimension | Continuity | Transformation |
|-----------|------------|---------------|
| Productivity | Stagnant [(41)](#ref41) | Via specialization [(40)](#ref40) |
| Trajectory | Temporary [(41)](#ref41) | Permanent shift [(44)](#ref44) |

The Black Death left Europe oriented toward capital-intensive, labor-saving production—a path culminating in industrialization.

## 8. Historiographical Debates and Open Questions

> Three schools (Malthusian, Marxist, Brenner) define the field; modern synthesis merges their insights.

### 8.1 Major Schools

Three schools dominate: (1) Malthusian/Postan — demographic cycles drive outcomes; (2) Marxist (Hilton, Bois) — class struggle is primary; (3) Brenner — class relations trump demography [(42)](#ref42).

| School | Driver | Proponents |
|--------|--------|------------|
| Malthusian | Demography | Postan, Hatcher [(42)](#ref42) |
| Marxist | Class struggle | Hilton, Bois [(42)](#ref42) |
| Brenner | Class relations | Brenner [(43)](#ref43) |

The Brenner Debate (1976) was the pivotal Marxist–Malthusian clash [(43)](#ref43). Postan: BD was a "golden age of the labourer" — wages doubled, serfdom ended [(44)](#ref44). Marxist counter: elites extracted surplus via state power; the transition was class struggle [(42)](#ref42).

| View | Wages | Institutions |
|------|-------|--------------|
| Postan [(44)](#ref44) | Doubled | Serfdom fell |
| Marxist [(42)](#ref42) | Extracted | Class transition |

### 8.2 Cross-Perspective

Modern work synthesizes both. Jedwab, Johnson & Koyama see BD as an exogenous shock that raised wages, cut inequality, and reshaped institutions — Postan in the short run, Marxist in the long run [(43)](#ref43).

| Horizon | Wage Story | Institution Story |
|---------|------------|-------------------|
| Short-run | Rise [(44)](#ref44)[(43)](#ref43) | Adjustment |
| Long-run | Entrenched [(43)](#ref43) | Transition [(42)](#ref42)[(43)](#ref43) |

Open questions: Why did elite responses differ regionally? How fast did institutional change lock in? Micro wage data bridging macro narratives remains the frontier.
